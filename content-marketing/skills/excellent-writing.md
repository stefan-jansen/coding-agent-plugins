# Skill: Excellent Writing

**Purpose**: Concrete, measurable criteria for excellent writing across all formats
**Status**: Core quality standard for content-marketing plugin
**Application**: Editorial review, quality metrics, revision guidance

---

## Core Principle

**Less is more.** Every word must earn its place. Trust reader intelligence. Information density over volume.

---

## The 15 Principles

### Principle 1: No Filler Words

**Rule**: Remove words that add no information

**Examples**:
- ❌ "It's really important to note that..."
- ✅ "Note that..." or just state the point
- ❌ "In order to achieve this goal"
- ✅ "To achieve this"
- ❌ "At this point in time"
- ✅ "Now"

**Check**: Can you delete the word without losing meaning? Delete it.

---

### Principle 2: Information-Dense Sentences

**Rule**: Every sentence adds new information. No repetition, no fluff.

**Examples**:
- ❌ "The framework is useful. It helps with development. Development becomes faster."
- ✅ "The framework accelerates development."
- ❌ "This is a critical point. It's important because it affects outcomes."
- ✅ "This affects outcomes." (criticality implied by stating it)

**Check**: Does this sentence tell the reader something they don't already know from previous sentences?

---

### Principle 3: Context Without Preamble

**Rule**: Provide background and motivation efficiently, then get to the point.

**Examples**:
- ❌ "Before we dive into the solution, let's first understand the problem space and why this matters..."
- ✅ "The problem: [specific issue]. The solution: [specific approach]."
- ❌ "In this section, we'll explore how..."
- ✅ Just explore it (section heading already signals topic)

**Check**: Can you provide context in fewer words? Do it.

---

### Principle 4: Economy of Language

**Rule**: Shorter words beat longer words. Fewer words beat more words.

**Examples**:
- ❌ "utilize" → ✅ "use"
- ❌ "facilitate" → ✅ "enable" or "help"
- ❌ "in close proximity to" → ✅ "near"
- ❌ "at the present time" → ✅ "now"
- ❌ "due to the fact that" → ✅ "because"

**Check**: Is there a shorter word or phrase that means the same thing? Use it.

---

### Principle 5: One Example Suffices

**Rule**: If one clear example makes the point, don't add more.

**Examples**:
- ❌ "For example, X. Another example is Y. You could also do Z."
- ✅ "For example, X." (then move on)
- Multiple examples OK when: Showing range/variety is the point

**Check**: Does the second example add new information, or reinforce the same point?

---

### Principle 6: Neutral Questions

**Rule**: Questions should be genuinely neutral, not telegraph the answer.

**Examples**:
- ❌ "Isn't it obvious that X is better?"
- ✅ "Which approach is better: X or Y?"
- ❌ "Don't you think we should..."
- ✅ "Should we..."

**Check**: Could you answer the question with either yes or no without sounding contrarian?

---

### Principle 7: No Restating Premise

**Rule**: After introducing a concept, don't explain why it matters. Trust reader understands from context.

**Examples**:
- ❌ "File-based state management stores all state in files. This approach is important because it prevents state corruption."
- ✅ "File-based state management stores all state in files, preventing corruption."
- ❌ "The positioning manifest constrains execution. This matters because it keeps content focused."
- ✅ "The positioning manifest constrains execution, keeping content focused."

**Check**: Does this sentence just repeat what the reader should already understand? Cut it.

---

### Principle 8: Cut Transitional Fluff

**Rule**: Avoid sentences that announce importance or benefits. Jump to actionable content.

**Examples**:
- ❌ "Understanding X helps you Y. Let's explore X..."
- ✅ "X enables Y: [explanation]"
- ❌ "This is important because..."
- ✅ Just state what makes it important
- ❌ "Let's take a closer look at..."
- ✅ Just look at it

**Check**: Is this sentence setup or substance? Cut setup, keep substance.

---

### Principle 9: Combine Related Ideas

**Rule**: Merge related statements instead of repeating concepts.

**Examples**:
- ❌ "X is important. X helps with Y. Here's how X works..."
- ✅ "X helps with Y: [how it works]"
- ❌ "The framework uses state files. State files persist between sessions. This enables resumption."
- ✅ "The framework's state files persist between sessions, enabling resumption."

**Check**: Are you making multiple statements about the same concept? Combine them.

---

### Principle 10: Trust Reader Intelligence

**Rule**: Don't over-explain obvious implications. Assume competence.

**Examples**:
- ❌ "Use shorter variable names. This will make your code more readable because shorter names are easier to scan."
- ✅ "Use shorter variable names for readability."
- ❌ "The system failed. This means it stopped working and needs to be fixed."
- ✅ "The system failed and needs fixing."

**Check**: Would an intelligent reader find this condescending? Cut it.

---

### Principle 11: Start Sections With Meat

**Rule**: Lead with specific advice, not general statements about importance or benefits.

**Examples**:
- ❌ "Understanding configuration is critical. Configuration affects behavior. Here's how to configure..."
- ✅ "Configure X by: [specific steps]"
- ❌ "Quality metrics are essential for maintaining standards. They help ensure..."
- ✅ "Quality metrics: [list specific metrics]"

**Check**: Does the first sentence give actionable information? If not, delete setup sentences.

---

### Principle 12: Simple Punctuation

**Rule**: Use periods, commas, colons instead of em dashes unless truly needed for emphasis or clarity.

**Examples**:
- ❌ "The framework—which uses files—enables resumption."
- ✅ "The framework uses files and enables resumption."
- ✅ "The framework (using files) enables resumption." (if parenthetical needed)
- OK: "The framework uses files—this matters." (em dash for emphasis)

**Check**: Can you replace the em dash with a period, comma, or colon? Do it.

---

### Principle 13: Cut Qualifying Phrases

**Rule**: Remove phrases that don't add concrete information.

**Examples**:
- ❌ "if you focus on the right features"
- ✅ Just state which features
- ❌ "when done correctly"
- ✅ Either explain how or trust reader knows
- ❌ "with proper configuration"
- ✅ State what proper configuration means

**Check**: Does this phrase add specific information or just hedge? If hedging, cut it.

---

### Principle 14: Direct Statements

**Rule**: State what things do, not that they're important.

**Examples**:
- ❌ "X is important—here's why: [explanation]"
- ✅ "X does: [what it does]"
- ❌ "Understanding Y is critical because it enables..."
- ✅ "Y enables..."
- ❌ "This concept matters for several reasons"
- ✅ "This concept affects: [specific impacts]"

**Check**: Are you talking *about* the importance, or stating the substance? State substance.

---

### Principle 15: Remove Setup Phrases

**Rule**: Delete phrases like "It's worth noting that" or "The key point is" and state the point directly.

**Examples**:
- ❌ "It's worth noting that the framework uses files."
- ✅ "The framework uses files."
- ❌ "The key point is that positioning comes first."
- ✅ "Positioning comes first."
- ❌ "What's interesting here is..."
- ✅ Just state the interesting thing

**Check**: Is this phrase a wrapper around the real statement? Unwrap it.

## Application Guidelines

### During Drafting

**Author agent applies principles**:
- Write information-dense sentences (Principle 2)
- Use economy of language (Principle 4)
- Start sections with meat (Principle 11)
- Direct statements over importance claims (Principle 14)

### During Editorial Review

**Editor agent checks violations**:
- Flag filler words (Principle 1)
- Identify repeated information (Principle 2)
- Mark transitional fluff (Principle 8)
- Spot setup phrases (Principle 15)

### During Revision

**Author agent addresses feedback**:
- Cut flagged filler
- Combine related ideas (Principle 9)
- Replace em dashes (Principle 12)
- Remove qualifying phrases (Principle 13)

## Quality Metrics

### Measurable Checks

**Automated detection possible**:
- Filler word count (Principle 1): "really", "very", "actually", "basically", etc.
- Em dash frequency (Principle 12): Flag excessive use
- Setup phrases (Principle 15): "It's worth noting", "The key point is", "What's interesting"
- Qualifying phrases (Principle 13): "when done correctly", "if you focus on the right"

**Manual review required**:
- Information density (Principle 2): Check for repetition
- Combined ideas (Principle 9): Look for related statements that could merge
- Section starts (Principle 11): First sentence gives actionable content?
- Direct statements (Principle 14): Substance stated, not importance?

### Pass Threshold

**Excellent writing passes**:
- Zero or minimal filler words
- No repeated information
- No transitional fluff
- Sections start with meat
- Direct statements throughout
- Simple punctuation
- No setup phrases

**Good writing** may have minor violations but overall follows principles

**Needs revision** violates multiple principles frequently

## Format Adaptations

### Long-Form (White Papers, Primers)

**All 15 principles apply fully**

**Emphasis**:
- Information density (Principle 2): Critical for sustained attention
- Economy of language (Principle 4): Keeps long pieces readable
- Section starts (Principle 11): Helps readers navigate
- No restating premise (Principle 7): Avoids redundancy across sections

### Social Media (LinkedIn, Twitter)

**All 15 principles apply, with adaptations**

**Emphasis**:
- Economy of language (Principle 4): Character limits demand efficiency
- One example suffices (Principle 5): Space constraints
- Direct statements (Principle 14): Attention constraints
- Remove setup phrases (Principle 15): Every character counts

**Adaptation**: Em dashes OK for emphasis in short-form (Principle 12 relaxed)

### Website Copy (Landing Pages, Hubs)

**All 15 principles apply fully**

**Emphasis**:
- Start sections with meat (Principle 11): Users scan, not read
- Direct statements (Principle 14): Clear value proposition
- Cut qualifying phrases (Principle 13): Confidence matters
- Trust reader intelligence (Principle 10): Don't talk down

### Code Examples

**Principles 1-4, 7-15 apply**

**Exemption**: Multiple examples OK when showing variety is the point (Principle 5 relaxed)
